## Design Context

### Users

**Primary: players.** Story-first folks who want to *inhabit* a persistent, shared fantasy world. Their first visit is browsing — at home, on a couch, often on a phone, sometimes late at night while their head is already in the world. They are not buying a tool; they are deciding whether this place is one they want to belong to. The page should feel like the cover and first plate of an atlas they want to keep on their shelf.

**Secondary: game masters.** Foundry VTT users who'll arrive looking for proof: does the map canvas module actually work, what does it produce, can I use it in tomorrow's session. They scan; they read captions; they need the tool pages to deliver concrete value fast.

**Tertiary: AI tinkerers.** People drawn in by the MCP server and the OHM stack. They need technical legitimacy signals (real endpoints, real repos, real tags) but they are not the audience the homepage is being designed *for*.

**Quaternary: investors / patrons.** Patreon backers and potential supporters. The site should make the project look serious and ongoing without ever pivoting into a pitch deck.

### Brand Personality

**Three words: crafted, warm, expeditionary.**

The voice is an **open-source explorers' guild** — a small research lab that happens to love dragons. Confident, not solemn. Slightly self-effacing ("Serious tech for non-serious worlds" is the brand's own joke about itself, and the design should be in on it). Reads like the annotated journal of a cartographic expedition: precise, opinionated, human-authored, with marginalia.

What it is NOT: a SaaS platform, a corporate product page, a metal album cover, a children's fantasy storybook, a Hugo-default theme.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme: paper page, instrument-panel maps.** The body of the site lives on a warm paper surface — calm, generous reading. Map and tool surfaces (canvas previews, the MCP server section, the FoundryVTT module demo) are framed in deep, dark "chart room" panels so the maps themselves get to glow. This earns its dark mode by giving it a job; it avoids the AI-default dark page; it doesn't retreat to a safe all-light layout either.

**Reference vocabulary** (mood, not pastiche):
- Old USGS topographic sheets and naval charts — the legend, the scale bar, the printed plate number in the corner
- Edward Tufte's books — small multiples, marginalia, captions that earn their keep
- Field-research journals — handwritten-feeling annotations next to typeset body text
- Atlas Obscura and the better Wikipedia infoboxes — confident asymmetry, photo + caption + coordinates

**Anti-references** (explicitly off-limits):
- Generic SaaS / AI startup: soft purple-to-blue gradients, sans-serif card grids, hero-with-screenshot, glowing CTAs
- Heavy-metal fantasy clichés: blackletter dragons-and-skulls, blood spatter, faux-medieval flourishes
- Hugo / Ananke default silhouette: full-bleed image header + black nav bar + centered prose column

### Design Principles

1. **Players first; lore is the headline.** Lead every page with the *world*, not the tech stack. Technical credibility appears as proof underneath — endpoints, repos, tags — never as the lede. If a page can't be understood by someone who has never written code, it's wrong.

2. **The page is a printed plate.** Treat layouts like pages from an atlas or field journal: room for marginalia, captions, plate numbers, scale indicators, coordinates. Reject the centered-hero / 3-card-grid silhouette by default. Confident asymmetry; left-aligned, generous gutter; a single accent pigment used sparingly like a cartographer's red ink.

3. **Maps glow; prose breathes.** Body content lives on warm off-white (paper) with dark warm-brown ink. Map, tool, and demo surfaces are framed in deep teal-navy "chart room" panels — that's where color and motion are allowed to concentrate. The contrast between the two surfaces is the site's signature.

4. **Every ornament has a job.** Decorative elements must double as information: a coordinate, a compass rose pointing to the next section, a printed plate number, a torn-edge that marks "this is a sample." No purely decorative gradients, drop shadows, glassmorphism, or icon-above-heading templates. If you can't name what an element communicates, delete it.

5. **Don't be in on the joke too loudly.** The "Serious tech for non-serious worlds" tagline is the only place the design is allowed to wink at the user. Everything else plays it straight, like a real atlas. Restraint is what makes the project feel real, and what separates it from every generic fantasy site.

### Typography Direction

- **Display / headings**: a humanist serif with character — *not* Crimson, Fraunces, Newsreader, Playfair, Cormorant, or any of the reflex serifs. Strong candidates: **Spectral** (Production Type for Google, screen-tuned, has presence in italic), **Eczar**, **Vollkorn**, or **Bitter** if a slab register fits the page.
- **Body**: a warm, low-contrast humanist sans — **Alegreya Sans** is a strong default; consider **Public Sans** or **Figtree** if more neutrality is wanted. *Not* Inter, DM Sans, Plus Jakarta, Outfit, Manrope.
- **Mono / accents** (for coordinates, plate numbers, code): **Departure Mono** (designed to look like an old technical manual — perfect for this voice) or **Commit Mono**. *Not* IBM Plex Mono, Space Mono, JetBrains Mono.
- Use mono only where it carries information (coordinates, IDs, code). Never as decorative "this is technical" paint.

### Color Direction

OKLCH throughout. Tint all neutrals toward the brand hue (warm red-orange, ~30°).

- **Paper surface** (page background): warm off-white, ~`oklch(97% 0.012 85)`
- **Ink** (body text): warm dark brown, ~`oklch(22% 0.02 50)`. Never pure black.
- **Accent — cinnabar** (used sparingly, like cartographer's red): ~`oklch(58% 0.17 32)`. Reserved for one or two marks per screen: a section indicator, a hover state, a key route on a map.
- **Chart-room surface** (map / tool / demo panels): deep teal-navy, ~`oklch(22% 0.04 230)`, with paper-tinted text on top.
- **Hairlines & rules**: warm gray tinted toward paper, ~`oklch(85% 0.008 80)`. Hairlines, not heavy borders. Never `border-left: 4px solid` accent stripes.
